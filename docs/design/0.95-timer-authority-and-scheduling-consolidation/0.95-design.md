# Canic 0.95 Design: Timer Authority And Scheduling Consolidation

Date: 2026-07-20

## Status

- Status: accepted and active.
- Release line: 0.95, anchored to released `v0.94.14` after the bounded 0.94
  backup/restore line closed.
- Scope: every autonomous canister timer, lifecycle deferral, retry scheduler,
  and shared timer primitive in this repository.
- Bounded command-scoped host waits are inventoried but remain outside the
  canister timer runtime.
- Receipt replay horizons, receipt-retention state, terminal reclamation,
  capacity engineering, and Toko adoption are explicitly deferred to the
  [0.96 receipt proposal](../0.96-receipt-replay-horizon-and-terminal-reclamation/0.96-design.md).
- Slice A inventory and every finding disposition are frozen in the
  [timer-authority audit](../../audits/reports/2026-07/2026-07-20/0.95-timer-authority-slice-a.md).
  Slice B common-authority implementation is authorized; later owner slices
  remain finding-backed by that inventory.
- The maintainer-approved duration amendment in this document supersedes only
  the audit's inherited cadence and fixed-retry choices. Process identity,
  ownership, batch bounds, and finding dispositions remain frozen.

## Decision

0.95 gives Canic one timer authority and migrates the existing timer owners to
it. The line is complete when every current process has a justified trigger,
cannot overlap itself, reconstructs correctly after lifecycle changes, reports
truthful status, and remains unregistered when event- or deadline-driven work
is absent.

The release may add a generic bounded due-work primitive. It does not add a
receipt-specific record, deadline, eligibility index, capacity counter, API,
or downstream contract.

The dependency between release lines is:

```text
0.95 releases the audited timer authority and bounded due-work scheduler
  -> 0.96 consumes that scheduler for receipt terminal reclamation
```

Unresolved Toko retry, authorization, rate, and entrypoint decisions cannot
block 0.95 closeout.

## Release Boundary

0.95 owns:

- exhaustive timer and timed-wait inventory;
- the one direct IC timer platform boundary;
- guarded one-shot registration and replacement;
- execution non-overlap and running-state arbitration;

- two-dimensional live timer status and fixed-cardinality metrics;
- removal of unnecessary idle polling;
- migration of existing autonomous process owners; and
- the pre-1.0 public timer-surface hard cut.

0.96 owns:

- downstream authorization and operation binding;
- receipt replay metadata and eligibility indexes;

- receipt-record and stable-memory capacity proof;
- receipt capacity diagnostics; and
- the Toko adoption contract.

0.95 must not add a placeholder receipt API or stable allocation in
anticipation of 0.96.

## Current Implementation Reality

### Shared timer infrastructure

Production direct calls to `ic_cdk_timers` are contained in
`ops::runtime::timer`, which is the correct platform boundary. Above it,
`TimerWorkflow`, `TimerApi`, and public timer macros repeat or expose scheduling
choices.

Slice A reproduces these findings and admits them to implementation:

- guarded registration prevents duplicate handles but does not prevent two
  async interval callbacks from overlapping;
- guarded one-shots depend on callback-specific manual slot clearing;
- guarded scheduling and slot handoff are repeated across ops and API layers;
- lifecycle deferrals call `TimerApi`, `TimerWorkflow`, and `TimerOps` from
  different adapters despite having the same purpose;
- the public facade returns opaque handles without a maintained cancellation
  operation;
- timer metrics are keyed by scheduling history, while status projects those
  historical rows as enabled and registered after a one-shot completes or a
  timer is cleared; and
- timer tasks return `()`, preventing the common layer from distinguishing
  success, no work, retry, continuation, or expected failure.

### Built-in process inventory

Slice A confirms these production process families and freezes their
dispositions.

The public timer macros are capability surfaces, not built-in processes. The
initial call-site scan finds in-repository runtime-probe use of unguarded
one-shot and interval macros but no production consumer of the guarded public
macros. Every retained macro and facade method requires a current consumer and
complete semantics; otherwise 0.95 removes it.

### Host timed-wait boundary

Root-install readiness polling and backup command-lifetime quiescence are
bounded process waits, not autonomous canister timers. Test-only sleep loops do
not become production inventory rows unless they specify production behavior.

The audit records host waits so time-based behavior is not hidden, but it does
not route them through the canister scheduler. Backup process-death and
quiescence work remains owned by 0.94.

## Audit Contract

The audit covers production Rust under `crates/`, `canisters/`, and `fleets/`.
It searches direct timer calls, wrappers, macros, lifecycle registration,
self-rescheduling callbacks, retry delays, sleeps, intervals, and timeout
loops.

Every inventory row records:

1. fixed timer key and diagnostic label;
2. owning role and configuration gate;
3. lifecycle registration and reconstruction path;
4. current scheduling mode, initial delay, cadence, retry delay, and batch
   bound, plus the accepted duration origin, formula, and operational bound;
5. authoritative work predicate and whether it is heap or stable;
6. awaited or external effects and overlap risk;
7. cursor, continuation, retry, and stop conditions;
8. idle wake-up and instruction/stable-memory cost;
9. expected failure result and operator visibility;
10. public and test consumers; and
11. accepted disposition: retain, remove, event-drive, deadline-drive,
    consolidate, or block for evidence.

The dated audit report contains command-backed source evidence and negative
searches. A new direct timer call, wrapper bypass, or production sleep loop
must make the inventory guard fail until assigned to an owner.

The decision rules are:

- use an event when Canic observes every transition that creates work;
- use an exact deadline when authoritative state identifies the earliest due
  item;
- use a retry wake-up only while external or fallible work remains pending;
- use recurring observation only when relevant state changes without a local
  event or maintained deadline;
- use a zero-delay one-shot only for a lifecycle deferral or bounded
  continuation boundary;
- derive a delay from authoritative state whenever possible; otherwise tie it
  to an explicit recovery or safety objective and measure it;
- disarm when the durable work predicate becomes false; and
- reject a timer whose only justification is that an older timer existed.

Timer removal requires proof that lifecycle reconstruction, concurrent
producers, and retries cannot lose work. Timer retention requires a named work
reason, bounded callback, non-overlap guarantee, reconstruction rule, measured
cost, and truthful status.

## Duration Selection Policy

Timer duration is domain policy, not a convenient implementation constant.
Every delay must have exactly one of these origins:

| Duration origin | Rule |
| --- | --- |
| Semantic zero | Permitted only for lifecycle deferral, newly observed work, or yielding bounded continuation through another message. |
| Authoritative deadline | Compute the exact earliest due timestamp from stable domain state. State changes may move it earlier or later. |
| Retry backoff | Exists only while typed recoverable work remains. The owner defines deterministic growth, a cap, and the event that resets or advances it. |
| Safety observation | Permitted only when relevant state can change without a local event or deadline. Its maximum interval is an explicit operational objective backed by cost and failure-window evidence. |
| Application supplied | Public application timers retain the caller's duration; Canic owns only non-overlap, cancellation, and status truth. |

Every built-in owner has one compact duration decision record before its
migration begins:

```text
timer_key:
duration_origin:
authoritative_inputs:
formula_or_deadline:
minimum_delay_and_reason:
maximum_delay_and_maximum_harm:
advance_events:
reset_events:
idle_callbacks_per_day:
failure_window:
measurement_or_test_evidence:
```

`minimum_delay` prevents useless rapid retries; `maximum_delay` bounds how
long required work may remain unattended. Neither is inherited from the old
implementation. If a deadline or external `retry_after` supplies one bound,
the record says so instead of inventing another number. 0.95 does not add
configuration knobs solely to make these values tunable.

For all built-in scheduling:

- a deadline at or before the current time becomes bounded immediate work;
- `RetryAfter` and `RecurAfter` must be greater than zero;
- deadline and backoff arithmetic is checked and fails closed on overflow;
- continuation may use zero delay only after observable bounded progress;
- backoff cannot cross a domain safety deadline; and
- jitter is not added without evidence of correlated fleet retries.

The following are not valid duration origins:

- an older implementation used the same number;
- a round minute or hour is convenient;
- several unrelated owners can share one wake-up; or
- a local invariant failure might repair itself if polled indefinitely.

Expected external failures use owner-specific deterministic backoff. New
authoritative work or evidence may advance a backoff deadline immediately.
Successful progress resets the backoff. Local stable-state contradictions,
schema failures, arithmetic failures, and other invariant causes do not
self-retry: they stop in `unregistered + failed` with durable work and typed
diagnostics preserved for lifecycle or operator recovery.

### Owner duration ledger

| Owner | Accepted duration rule |
| --- | --- |
| Lifecycle and user hooks | Zero delay is semantic: synchronous invariants are restored and the next phase must yield into asynchronous execution. One invocation per lifecycle path. |
| Local-intent expiry | Exact first eligible expiry from the ordered index. Due batches continue at zero delay while bounded work remains. A local storage/invariant failure stops failed; it does not poll every minute. |
| Log retention | No timer for count/byte-only policy. With maximum age configured, schedule the exact oldest-entry expiry and bounded continuation. Local invariant failure stops failed. |
| Pool reset | No 30-minute maintenance cadence. Lifecycle and producers schedule immediate known work. Recoverable external failure uses deterministic capped backoff whose minimum and maximum are frozen from external-call cost and the maximum acceptable pool-recovery delay. New pool work advances the retry immediately. |
| Placement acknowledgement | Immediate for newly terminal receipts. Recoverable root/transport failure uses deterministic capped backoff whose maximum is proven against receipt retention and capacity pressure. Exact new evidence advances the deadline; success resets backoff. |
| Delegated-proof renewal | Exact minimum issuer refresh or `next_attempt_after` deadline. The auth owner, not the timer runtime, derives retry time from failure class and remaining proof validity. Retry bounds must preserve the proof safety margin and may not be copied from the current minute sweep. |
| Cycle top-up safety | Independent and registered only while automatic funding is enabled. Derive the next check from balance headroom, configured funding threshold, and observed burn rate, subject to measured minimum and maximum safety bounds. Known cycle-spending events may advance it. |
| Cycle history | Record lifecycle and funding observations without another timer. A standalone recurring sample is retained only if an explicit operator-resolution requirement is accepted; the public history query alone does not justify hourly writes. |

### Pool reset duration decision

```text
timer_key: pool:pending
duration_origin: semantic zero plus retry backoff
authoritative_inputs: stable pending-reset rows, ordered cursor, typed local importability result
formula_or_deadline: immediate for lifecycle/producer/continuation; min(60s * 2^blocked_sweeps, 30m) only for local non-importability
minimum_delay_and_reason: 60s; bound interactive local recovery without an immediate status-call loop
maximum_delay_and_maximum_harm: 30m; explicitly retain the current maximum discovery delay, but only while known local work remains
advance_events: queued import, failed recycle with a retained pending row, lifecycle reconstruction
reset_events: terminal progress or no pending rows
idle_callbacks_per_day: 0
failure_window: first retry within 60s; sustained local-only blockage checked at least every 30m
measurement_or_test_evidence: 0 idle callbacks; at most 52 callbacks for one blocked <=10-row sweep in its first 24h and 48/day at the cap
```

`NonImportableOnLocal` is produced only by the local-build management-status
probe, so this retry cannot consume production IC cycles. Other policy variants
at this boundary are invariant contradictions and stop failed. Reset command
failure already becomes durable terminal `Failed` pool evidence and does not
remain in the retry queue. No separate maintenance timer survives.

### Placement acknowledgement duration decision

```text
timer_key: placement:receipt_ack
duration_origin: semantic zero plus retry backoff
authoritative_inputs: lifecycle-rebuilt terminal placement index, 32-row callback bound, root retained-receipt ceiling of 512 per caller, typed root call result
formula_or_deadline: immediate for lifecycle/producer/continuation; min(60s * 2^failed_attempts, 30m) after the first root transport/ops failure
minimum_delay_and_reason: 60s; one failed root call ends the attempt, avoiding an immediate inter-canister retry loop during a root-wide outage
maximum_delay_and_maximum_harm: 30m; without newer placement evidence, one retained receipt may occupy root replay capacity for at most 30m after root recovery
advance_events: newly terminal placement evidence, lifecycle reconstruction, bounded successful-page continuation
reset_events: any successful acknowledgement/removal or an empty index
idle_callbacks_per_day: 0
failure_window: immediate first attempt; transport recovery observed within 1m initially and at least every 30m under sustained failure
measurement_or_test_evidence: at most 52 failed root calls in the first 24h and 48/day at the cap; healthy 512-row caller ceiling drains in at most 16 immediate 32-row callbacks
```

Only `Infra` and `Ops` root-call failures retry. A public root rejection means
the retained local identity contradicts root authority; local stable/index
failure is likewise an invariant. Both stop failed with exact terminal evidence
retained for lifecycle or operator recovery. A retrying attempt stops on its
first failed root call because transport availability is root-wide; it does not
spend another 31 calls before applying backoff. New durable placement work may
advance the retry to semantic zero but does not erase the failure streak;
successful acknowledgement does.

### Log-retention duration and storage decision

```text
timer_key: log_retention:run
duration_origin: exact oldest-entry age deadline
authoritative_inputs: configured max_age_secs, oldest retained created_at, append-owned max_entries/max_entry_bytes, 256-row callback bound
formula_or_deadline: oldest.created_at + max_age_secs + 1s, preserving the existing strict created_at < now - max_age_secs boundary
minimum_delay_and_reason: semantic zero only when entries are already due or another bounded due page remains
maximum_delay_and_maximum_harm: the configured age itself plus the one-second strict-boundary resolution; no unrelated observation interval remains
advance_events: first retained append, count eviction that changes the oldest entry, lifecycle reconstruction, bounded due-page continuation
reset_events: every append reconciles the sole exact deadline; empty storage or max_age_secs unset makes the owner idle
idle_callbacks_per_day: 0
failure_window: local configuration/storage contradictions stop failed; no polling retry is justified
measurement_or_test_evidence: default max_age_secs unset reduces 144 callbacks/day to zero; one callback removes at most 256 expired rows
```

The Slice A statement that count and byte enforcement were both append-owned
was inaccurate. The final Slice C correction gives them one real authority:
append truncates the entry and evicts the single displaced oldest row before
returning. Age retention alone uses the timer and deletes at most 256 ordered
rows per message. It never clears and rewrites retained history.

The old append-only two-memory log cannot remove an oldest row without a full
rewrite, so allocations 31 and 32 and their `log_index`/`log_data` state
descriptions are hard-cut. Allocation 35 is the sole current ordered runtime-
log map and is declared as the `runtime_log` state domain. Existing runtime-log
history is non-authoritative diagnostic data and is not migrated or read
through a compatibility path. Lowering `max_entries` below the currently
retained count likewise clears that diagnostic history in one bounded hard cut
rather than running an unbounded deletion loop during upgrade.

### Cycle observation and automatic-top-up duration decision

```text
timer_key: cycles:topup
duration_origin: configured funding safety observation
authoritative_inputs: current balance, configured funding threshold, most recent balance observation, current timer expected-failure streak
burn_rate: max(ceil(observed_decrease / elapsed_seconds) * 2, ceil(configured_threshold / 24h)); observations older than 12h or without a net decrease use the threshold/24h floor
formula_or_deadline: projected threshold crossing minus 5m funding allowance minus 5m safety margin
minimum_delay_and_reason: 1m while above threshold and after one completed funding effect to prevent observation or repeated-success loops
maximum_delay_and_maximum_harm: 6h; an unobserved external spend can remain unseen for at most 6h while the balance is above threshold
insufficient_history_fallback: configured_threshold / 24h conservative burn floor, then the same headroom formula and bounds
at_or_below_threshold: semantic zero when newly observed; after one completed funding effect, wait the 1m minimum before another bounded attempt
expected_failure_retry: 1m, 2m, 4m, 8m, 16m, then 30m cap from the common timer failure streak
advance_events: lifecycle reconstruction and completion of a funding attempt; no independent history tick exists
reset_events: successful funding resets the common expected-failure streak and derives a new deadline from the observed post-funding balance
idle_callbacks_per_day: 0 when automatic funding is disabled
failure_window: transport/ops failures retry within the bounded progression; public policy rejection, configuration contradiction, or non-resumable root refill outcome stops failed
measurement_or_test_evidence: the previous owner coupled all roles to 24 callbacks/day and made configured root refill unreachable; disabled roles now have zero callbacks and configured roles have one independently observable, non-overlapping funding owner
```

The scheduling formula is:

```text
headroom = current_balance - configured_threshold
projected_threshold_crossing = now + headroom / conservative_burn_rate
latest_safe_check = projected_threshold_crossing
  - worst_case_funding_completion_time
  - accepted_safety_margin
next_delay = clamp(
  latest_safe_check - now,
  minimum_check_spacing,
  maximum_safety_interval,
)
next_check = now + next_delay
```

This calculation applies only while balance is above the threshold. At or
below the threshold, known work is scheduled immediately. The conservative
rate doubles an observed net burn and never falls below one configured
threshold per day. A deposit, a flat/increasing balance, an observation with
no elapsed time, or an observation older than 12 hours uses that floor. This
is a scheduling bound, not a promise that linear burn predicts all external
spending.

Cycle history has no standalone timer. Every role records one lifecycle
observation. Configured top-up records its pre-effect observation and its
post-effect observation, so operator history follows actual observation and
funding boundaries. The maintained seven-day retention window is unchanged;
one observation removes at most 128 expired tracker rows and 128 expired
top-up-event rows. Remaining diagnostic history is eligible at the next
observation rather than creating a retention-only callback. The public history
query does not by itself justify recurring writes.

Nonroot top-up calls the existing parent-funding request. Root top-up is
enabled only by `topup.icp_refill.enabled` and awaits the existing hub
self-refill workflow. The timer callback awaits either effect directly, so the
common timer's running state is the sole non-overlap authority. The retired
heap in-flight flags, spawned detached funding task, coupled tracking mode, and
`cycles:tracking` timer identity have no compatibility path.

For delegated-proof renewal, the latest retry is bounded by the active proof's
expiry minus the accepted successful-renewal window. An issuer-provided or
typed `next_attempt_after` may choose an earlier retry. With no usable active
proof, the process reports the availability failure honestly and uses only the
bounded recovery policy accepted for that state; it must not imply that a
future timer makes login currently available.

Before Slice D implements cycle scheduling, its evidence note must freeze the
burn-rate estimator, insufficient-history fallback, minimum check spacing,
maximum safety interval, and behavior at or below the threshold. The current
hour is not inherited as a default. If automatic funding is disabled and no
standalone history-resolution requirement is accepted, cycle observation is
unregistered after its lifecycle sample.

## Central Timer Invariant

Every armed timer must correspond to a named lifecycle deferral, known due
work, pending retry, or necessary recurring observation.

For every timer key:

- at most one callback invocation runs at a time;
- scheduling while running cannot arm another runnable callback;
- each invocation performs bounded work and yields between continuation
  batches;
- authoritative subsystem state, not the heap handle, reconstructs work after
  upgrade;
- expected failures return typed outcomes;
- every non-application duration exposes its accepted origin and owner;
- no-work and disabled processes become unregistered; and
- status distinguishes registration truth from process condition.

This invariant applies to common scheduling mechanics. It does not move domain
work predicates, storage records, retry policy, or outcome interpretation into
the timer runtime.

## Shared Scheduling Architecture

### Fixed timer keys

Every built-in process has one fixed internal timer key. Labels and metrics are
derived from that key rather than caller-provided strings. A subsystem may
request scheduling for its key but cannot access another subsystem's handle or
running state.

The common runtime owns:

- IC handle creation and clearing;
- generation and request-sequence counters;
- registration and running arbitration;
- pending requested deadlines;
- after-completion scheduling;
- typed execution outcomes and scheduling directives; and
- live status and fixed-cardinality metrics.

The subsystem owns:

- whether work or observation is required;
- authoritative stable indexes, queues, and cursors;
- batch bounds;
- domain execution;
- expected error classification; and
- the returned next scheduling directive.

### Scheduling directives

Callbacks return one current directive:

```text
Stop
ContinueImmediately
RetryAfter { delay }
ScheduleAt { deadline }
RecurAfter { delay }
```

The execution outcome separately records success, no work, retryable failure,
or invariant failure. Expected domain failures are typed before they reach the
common runtime; error text never decides scheduling.

`ContinueImmediately` means a new zero-delay message after the current bounded
batch completes. It does not loop within the callback.

### Running-state arbitration

Registration non-duplication and execution non-overlap are separate
guarantees. The scheduler uses this exact rule:

```text
scheduled callback begins
  -> clear its consumed handle
  -> mark the timer key running for that generation
  -> while running, record scheduling requests as pending requirements
  -> do not arm any new callback for that key
  -> callback returns one typed outcome and directive
  -> merge the directive with pending requirements
  -> apply disable/cancel precedence
  -> mark the run complete
  -> arm at most one next handle
```

Pending schedule requests are stored as absolute deadlines. Multiple pending
requests merge to the earliest valid deadline.

Precedence at completion is:

1. a disable or cancel command issued during the run clears pending work and
   suppresses the callback's rearm directive;
2. a later explicit enable/schedule command supersedes that earlier
   disable/cancel but still waits for the running invocation to finish;
3. otherwise, an external pending requirement and the callback directive are
   merged and the earliest deadline wins; and
4. when neither requires a future run, the key becomes unregistered.

Commands carry a monotonic request sequence so "later" is deterministic. A
stale generation or earlier request cannot clear, arm, or overwrite a newer
decision. A request for earlier work while running therefore cannot overlap
the active callback, and it cannot be lost when that callback returns `Stop`.

### Guarded one-shots and recurrence

When a timer is scheduled but not running, an earlier requested deadline
replaces the later handle and advances the generation. A later request does not
postpone known earlier work unless an explicit policy command cancels or
reschedules it.

Guarded one-shots release their consumed handle in common infrastructure
before domain work begins. Subsystems do not clear their own slots.

Recurring async observation uses after-completion scheduling by default. Its
next delay begins only after the current invocation returns, preventing
overlap. A fixed-rate mode is permitted only if the audit proves a wall-clock
requirement and defines how missed ticks are coalesced without overlap.

### Generic bounded due work

0.95 may provide a reusable due-work runner with this contract:

```text
load authoritative earliest work
  -> stop or schedule its future deadline when nothing is due
  -> process at most the subsystem batch bound
  -> return typed outcome plus one scheduling directive
```

The primitive knows nothing about intents, receipts, proofs, logs, pools, or
acknowledgements. It does not own a stable record. A subsystem supplies the
ordered/queued authority and maps domain results into directives.

There is no shared minute or hour heartbeat. Independent timer keys retain
independent deadlines, failures, batches, and status.

## Timer Status And Observability

Timer status has two independent dimensions:

| Dimension | Values |
| --- | --- |
| Registration | `unregistered`, `scheduled`, `running` |
| Process condition | `disabled`, `idle`, `active`, `retrying`, `failed`, `missing_registration` |

Examples:

- an enabled queue with no work is `unregistered + idle`;
- a configured recurring observer between runs is `scheduled + active`;
- a retry wake-up is `scheduled + retrying`;
- disabled configuration is `unregistered + disabled`;
- authoritative due work without a handle is
  `unregistered + missing_registration`; and
- an invariant failure with automatic retry suppressed is
  `unregistered + failed`.

The live view reports at least:

- timer key, owner, scheduling mode, and enabled configuration;
- both status dimensions;
- current generation and next due timestamp when scheduled;
- last typed outcome and bounded work count;
- consecutive expected failures and retry deadline; and
- schedules, executions, successes, expected failures, and stale callbacks.

Last-run timestamps, generations, and execution counters are heap-only unless
an accepted finding proves durable operational value. Heap-only values are
explicitly labeled "since current runtime start" and reset on upgrade. They
must not be presented as lifetime history.

An ordered subsystem index remains the authority for its earliest deadline.
Status derives the deadline from the first key or the live handle; it does not
persist a duplicate `next_deadline` field unless measured evidence proves the
first-key read unsuitable.

Metric labels are fixed by timer key, registration state, process condition,
and bounded outcome class. Caller IDs, operation IDs, resource keys,
deadlines, and raw error strings are prohibited labels.

## Unexpected Traps

Typed outcomes cover expected failures only. 0.95 does not claim that the
runtime can catch and classify an unexpected canister trap after domain work
begins.

The contract is:

- expected failures must be returned as typed outcomes;
- an unexpected trap may prevent completion arbitration, failure recording,
  and next-handle registration;
- authoritative stable work remains the recovery source;
- live status may become overdue or `missing_registration` only when that
  condition can be established honestly;
- recovery occurs through a supported runtime restart path or lifecycle
  reconstruction; and
- 0.95 adds no watchdog heartbeat solely to recover trapped timers.

Each migrated owner must minimize the trap surface before its first await and
validate corrupt stable data as a typed invariant failure wherever possible.

## Lifecycle Reconstruction

Init and post-upgrade adapters restore synchronous Canic invariants, then use
one common zero-delay lifecycle deferral. Root, nonroot, and generated user
hooks have one documented ordering path.

Each subsystem reconstructs from its own authority:

- configured recurring observation schedules its next run;
- an ordered due-work owner schedules its earliest stable deadline;
- a queue owner schedules immediately only when stable work exists;
- a retry owner restores the accepted retry requirement; and
- an empty or disabled owner remains unregistered.

Reconstruction is idempotent in one runtime generation. Heap handles,
generations, last-run data, and execution counters are not imported as stable
truth.

Repeated start or configuration requests must merge through running-state
arbitration. An occupied slot cannot silently discard an earlier required run.

## Existing Owner Migration

### Lifecycle deferrals

Retain zero-delay behavior because lifecycle adapters cannot await. Replace
direct ops/workflow/API variation with one lifecycle scheduling facade and one
ordering test matrix.

### Local-intent expiry

Maintain a stable expiry-ordered index for finite-TTL intents. Begin, terminal
transition, abort, import, and removal update the index through intent ops.
TTL-free intents never enter it.

The workflow arms the earliest expiry, drains a bounded due batch, and stops
when the index is empty. It does not scan every pending intent each hour.

### Pool reset

Run one lifecycle bootstrap check. New or recovered pending work schedules the
bounded reset worker immediately. External or blocked work returns an accepted
deterministic backoff from the owner duration record. Empty stable state has no
pool timer. The current 30-minute maintenance interval is removed rather than
retained as a safety heartbeat; it does not silently become the retry cap.

### Placement acknowledgement

Use the event/retry pattern only while terminal acknowledgement work exists.
If the audit confirms that paging the receipt-backed primary map repeatedly
scans unrelated consumer records, introduce a placement-owned durable queue or
index. Recoverable external failure uses the owner duration ledger's bounded
backoff; invariant failure stops failed. The timer runtime does not interpret
acknowledgement results.

### Log retention

Do not remove the interval until all count, byte, and age policies have another
complete authority. The audit must evaluate:

- append-time enforcement for count and per-entry byte limits;
- exact earliest-age-expiry scheduling when maximum age is configured;
- whether an empty log or configuration without age policy can remain
  unregistered; and
- active-work instruction cost compared with the current bounded sweep.

The accepted design may retain a measured periodic sweep if an event/deadline
replacement is more complex or less reliable.

### Cycle sampling and automatic top-up

Cycle balance can change without a local event, so automatic-funding safety may
require recurring observation. That does not justify an independent hourly
history write. Sampling and top-up expose separate outcomes and process
status, and top-up is disabled and unregistered when configuration does not
enable it.

The top-up owner derives its next safety check from balance headroom and
observed burn under the duration ledger. History piggybacks on lifecycle and
funding observations unless a measured operator-resolution requirement admits
a separate cadence. Slice D freezes purge behavior, failure handling, and the
measured safety bounds before implementation.

### Delegated-proof renewal

Expiry requires time-based execution, not minute polling. Schedule the exact
minimum authoritative refresh or next-attempt deadline. Auth policy derives
retry timing from failure class and remaining validity under the duration
ledger. Configuration changes must advance an already scheduled later wake-up
through common arbitration. Renewal sweeps cannot overlap.

## Layering And Ownership

The strict dependency direction remains:

```text
endpoints -> workflow -> policy -> ops -> model
```

- `model::runtime` owns named internal timer keys and passive execution/status
  values where model placement is needed.
- `view::runtime` owns read-only timer projections.
- `ops::runtime::timer` is the only direct IC timer owner and performs
  mechanical handle operations and clock reads.
- `workflow::runtime::timer` owns common arbitration, generations,
  non-overlap, directives, recurrence, and live status.
- subsystem ops own authoritative stable indexes, queues, cursors, and
  conversion.
- subsystem workflows own work predicates, domain execution, batch bounds,
  and retry/recurrence decisions.
- lifecycle adapters and macros marshal and delegate immediately.
- policy remains pure and does not register timers, access storage, await, or
  inspect runtime status.

Workflow must not construct or mutate stable records. Timer ops must not learn
domain policy.

## Public And Stable Hard Cut

The timer facade changes directly to the accepted current surface. An unused
guarded macro, duplicate lifecycle entrypoint, opaque handle without a
maintained operation, or incomplete fixed-interval helper is removed when the
audit cannot prove a current consumer and complete semantics.

There are no aliases, deprecated wrappers, legacy fallbacks, or
anti-resurrection tests for removed forms. Current tests cover the maintained
surface only.

Schema versioning remains honest:

- a new stable allocation starts at schema version `1` when it requires a
  version;
- a changed existing versioned shape or meaning receives the next schema
  version even before product 1.0; and
- the previous reader is removed in the hard cut rather than retained as a
  compatibility path.

Product version and persisted-schema version are separate concepts. Current
unversioned internal types do not gain version suffixes merely because their
implementation changes.

Canonical export/import includes any accepted new authoritative expiry or
acknowledgement index. Heap timer handles, registry state, generations, and
runtime counters are reconstructed and are not canonical state.

## Risks And Mitigations

### Scheduling during a running callback overlaps

Mitigation: never arm while the key is running. Record pending absolute
deadlines, merge them after completion, apply sequenced disable/cancel
precedence, and arm at most one handle.

### Removing polling loses work

Mitigation: require authoritative work state plus lifecycle reconstruction for
every event/deadline migration. Retain recurring observation when relevant
state changes externally.

### After-completion recurrence drifts

Mitigation: use it for operational polling where non-overlap matters more than
wall-clock phase. Admit fixed-rate mode only with measured need and explicit
missed-tick coalescing.

### A global scheduler couples failures

Mitigation: share mechanics only. Timer keys, deadlines, domain workflows,
batches, and failures remain independent; no global heartbeat is introduced.

### Unexpected traps strand work

Mitigation: return expected failures, preserve stable work authority, expose
honest overdue/missing-registration evidence where possible, and recover only
through supported runtime/lifecycle paths. Do not claim automatic trap catch.

### DRY abstraction absorbs domain policy

Mitigation: the common runtime knows timer keys, requests, outcomes, and
directives only. Subsystem workflows and ops retain all domain decisions and
stable authority.

## Closed Decisions

- 0.95 is timer-only; receipt reclamation moves to 0.96.
- There is no global heartbeat.
- Durations come only from semantic zero, authoritative deadlines, bounded
  retry policy, explicit safety observation, or an application caller.
- Local invariant failures stop failed and never justify periodic self-retry.
- Direct IC timer access remains in one ops module.
- Scheduling while running records pending requirements and never arms another
  runnable callback.
- Disable/cancel precedence and later enable/schedule are request-sequenced.
- After-completion recurrence is the default for async recurring work.
- Timer status has independent registration and process-condition dimensions.
- Heap execution history is labeled "since current runtime start."
- Unexpected traps are not claimed as typed, automatically recovered failures.
- Ordered indexes own their earliest deadline; status does not persist a
  duplicate without evidence.
- Host command waits remain outside canister timer authority.
- Public removals are pre-1.0 hard cuts without compatibility layers.
- New persisted allocations begin at schema version `1`; changed existing
  versioned meanings receive the next schema version and lose the old reader.

## Slice A Frozen Decisions

1. Every audit row has the disposition recorded in the Slice A report.
2. The maintained application surface is `timer!`, `timer_interval!`, and one
   cancellation operation over the opaque handle. Guarded macros, `TimerSlot`,
   and public raw-CDK timer access are removed as hard cuts.
3. Request and generation sequences are checked heap `u64` values. Scheduling
   and cancellation commands carry a sequence, requests while running remain
   pending until completion, and checked overflow fails closed.
4. Log count and byte limits remain append-owned. Only configured age retention
   arms a timer, at the exact oldest expiry, with bounded continuation.
5. Placement acknowledgement receives a dedicated durable queue/index and no
   longer scans unrelated receipt-backed intents.
6. Cycle sampling and automatic top-up schedule independently. Configured
   nonroot top-up uses parent funding and configured root top-up uses the
   existing automatic ICP-refill workflow.
7. Delegated-proof renewal schedules the minimum authoritative next-attempt or
   refresh deadline. Auth policy derives bounded retry timing from the typed
   failure and remaining proof-validity window.
8. Per-owner batch and service bounds remain frozen in the Slice A report.
   Cadence and retry numbers are superseded by the maintainer-approved duration
   ledger above and require its owner-specific evidence.
9. Slice validation remains targeted; final closeout uses the cumulative gate
   already defined in this design.
